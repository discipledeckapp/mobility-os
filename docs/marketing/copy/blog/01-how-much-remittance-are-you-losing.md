# How Much Remittance Are You Actually Losing Every Month?

**Category:** Operations  
**Estimated read time:** 5 minutes  
**Target reader:** Fleet owner or manager running 20–100 vehicles on daily remittance

---

Most fleet operators know, in their gut, that they're not collecting everything they should. The driver says he paid. Your record says something different. Nobody can prove either way. And the next morning you're chasing the same argument again.

The money leaving through that gap is real. It's just hard to put a number on it — until you do the maths.

## Start with the numbers you already know

Let's say you're running 40 vehicles. Each vehicle is supposed to remit ₦10,000 a day. Over 26 operating days in a month, that's ₦260,000 per vehicle, or **₦10.4 million** across the fleet.

That's what the whiteboard says. Now ask yourself honestly: what actually lands in your hand?

Here's where most operators go quiet.

## The four places money disappears

**1. Shortfalls that go unrecorded**

The driver pays ₦7,000 instead of ₦10,000. He says the road was slow. You're already dealing with three other drivers queuing behind him. You wave him through, thinking you'll "sort it later." Later never comes.

Over a month, at just ₦1,500 per vehicle per day average shortfall — which is conservative — you're losing ₦1.56 million. On 40 vehicles.

**2. Disputes with no evidence**

The driver says he paid yesterday. You say he didn't. You're both standing there guessing. There's no record either way, so you either absorb the loss or damage the relationship — neither is good.

Without a timestamped record at the point of payment, disputes default to whoever is more confident. Usually the driver.

**3. Absent entries that don't get chased**

If your remittance record is a notebook or a WhatsApp group message, what happens when a driver simply doesn't show? He doesn't message. He doesn't pay. Three days pass. By day four, the outstanding amount has grown and he knows it, so he avoids your calls. The longer this runs, the less likely you are to recover anything.

**4. Assignment ghost payments**

A driver does two trips in a day and remits for one. There's no record of the second assignment — it was verbal, dispatched on WhatsApp, and no one followed up. You'll never know what you're owed.

## What 15% leakage looks like in naira

Industry estimates put remittance leakage in manual fleets at 10–20% per month. Take the middle ground — 15% — on our example above.

- Expected monthly: ₦10.4 million
- Leakage at 15%: **₦1.56 million per month**
- Per year: **₦18.72 million**

That's not a rounding error. That's a vehicle purchase. That's six months of payroll. That's the cost of keeping four or five vehicles on the road that you're currently losing to paper.

And this is a *conservative* estimate. On larger fleets with weaker tracking, operators have told us leakage runs past 20%.

## The harder truth: you can't fix what you can't see

Manual systems don't just lose money — they hide the losses. When every dispute is resolved by memory and every shortfall is handled informally, you can't tell which drivers are systematically underpaying, which routes are consistently short, or which days of the week your collection drops. You just see a number at the end of the month that's less than it should be.

The operators who close the gap fastest aren't necessarily the ones who collect harder. They're the ones who built a system where the record exists before the argument starts.

## What tightening remittance control actually looks like

Here's the shift that makes the difference:

- **Confirm or dispute at the point of collection**, not three days later
- **Every shortfall is logged** with a reason — so you can see patterns, not just incidents
- **Outstanding remittances surface automatically** — you don't have to remember to chase
- **Drivers know the record exists** — the accountability comes from the system, not from you having a better memory than they do

When drivers know that every payment is timestamped and every dispute is in the system, the calculation changes. It's not about catching people — it's about making the system clear enough that shortchanging it isn't worth the risk.

## Calculate your own number

Take your expected monthly remittance and multiply by 0.15. If that number makes you uncomfortable, that's the right feeling. The money isn't gone — it's bleeding through a gap that a system can close.
