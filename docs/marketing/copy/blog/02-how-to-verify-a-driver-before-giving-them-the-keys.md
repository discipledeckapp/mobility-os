# How to Verify a Driver Before You Hand Over the Keys

**Category:** Driver Management  
**Estimated read time:** 6 minutes  
**Target reader:** Fleet managers responsible for driver onboarding

---

Every experienced fleet operator has a version of the same story. A driver shows up with a reference. You call the number — it rings, someone vouches for them. The documents look right. You hand over the keys. Three weeks later, the vehicle is in Delta State and the driver isn't answering his phone.

You didn't do anything obviously wrong. You just didn't have a system that could tell you what you couldn't see.

Driver verification in Nigerian fleet operations is one of those things where the stakes are enormous and the tooling has been almost nonexistent. Most operators are working with photocopies, phone calls, and a gut feeling. That's not a criticism — it's the market reality. But it doesn't have to stay that way.

Here's what a proper verification process actually looks like, and why each step matters.

## Step 1: Capture identity before anything else

The first thing you need is a verified identity record — not a photocopy of an ID card, but a confirmed match between the person standing in front of you and the document they're presenting.

Documents to collect at minimum:

- **National Identification Number (NIN)** — the most important. NIN can be verified against the NIMC database to confirm the holder's name, date of birth, and photo.
- **Driver's Licence** — verify the licence number and expiry date. A licence that expired six months ago is a liability if that driver is stopped at an FRSC checkpoint.
- **Bank Verification Number (BVN)** — useful for financial accountability. BVN ties to a bank account and, by extension, a real identity in the banking system.
- **Utility bill or proof of address** — a current address is important if you ever need to locate a guarantor.

What you're doing at this stage is creating a record that can be checked. Not assumed. Not hoped about. Checked.

## Step 2: Get a face match

A document is not enough on its own. Documents are borrowed, shared, and sometimes forged. The step most operators skip — because it used to require specialist tools — is verifying that the face in front of you matches the face on the document.

This is what biometric onboarding solves. When a driver takes a selfie during onboarding, that image is compared against the photo on file for their NIN or licence. A mismatch is an immediate red flag. A match gives you confidence that the person is who they claim to be.

This matters even more in the age of identity fraud across transport. Drivers with a record at another operator sometimes reapply under a different name or with a borrowed NIN. Without a face check, there's no way to catch this.

## Step 3: Check the guarantor — properly

Guarantors in Nigerian fleet operations are almost always informal. The driver produces a family member or a friend. You meet them once. You take a phone number. If the driver absconds, you call the number and hope someone picks up.

This process fails at the point of stress — which is exactly when you need it to work.

A guarantor capture that actually functions requires:

- The guarantor's own verified identity (NIN at minimum)
- A signed, dated document making clear what they're liable for
- Contact details that are independently verified (not just provided by the driver)
- A record that exists in a system — not a folder that can be lost

Some operators go further and capture the guarantor's BVN. This creates a financial accountability link that is significantly harder to disappear from.

The guarantor shouldn't be an afterthought collected at the end of a rushed onboarding. It should be a structured process that the driver knows you take seriously — because that signals the driver's accountability starts before the first trip.

## Step 4: Check for cross-operator history

This is where most systems still have a gap — and where the stakes are highest.

A driver who absconded with a vehicle from another operator last year isn't going to list that employer as a reference. They're going to walk into your yard with a clean story and references from people who will vouch for them.

The only way to catch this is through a shared intelligence layer — a network of operators who have contributed to a driver history database that flags known incidents. When a new driver is onboarded, a search runs against that database. A match surfaces a risk signal, not necessarily a rejection, but a prompt to investigate further.

This exists in other markets. It's only beginning to exist in Nigeria, because until recently there was no platform to run it on.

## Step 5: Set up expiry tracking from day one

Verification isn't a one-time event. A driver whose licence is valid today will have an expired licence in two years. If you don't have a system tracking those expiry dates, you'll find out the wrong way — at a checkpoint, in an accident, or in a regulatory audit.

When you onboard a driver, the expiry dates on every captured document should go straight into a tracking system. You should receive an alert when something is 60 days from expiry. You should be able to see at a glance how many drivers across your fleet have documents expiring this quarter.

This is also what turns your compliance from reactive to proactive. Instead of scrambling when a regulator asks for documentation, you already know the answer.

## What this looks like when it's built properly

A structured onboarding flow takes a driver through identity capture, biometric verification, document upload, guarantor submission, and consent recording in a single session. The driver does most of it themselves, guided by the interface. The fleet manager gets a verified record at the end, not a folder of photocopies.

It typically takes 10–15 minutes. Compared to the paper-and-phone process most operators are running, it's faster — and it produces a record that holds up.

When the same driver tries to onboard at another fleet two months later with a different name and a similar face, the system already knows.
