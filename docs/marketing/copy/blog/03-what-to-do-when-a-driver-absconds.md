# What to Do When a Driver Absconds With Your Vehicle

**Category:** Driver Management, Risk  
**Estimated read time:** 7 minutes  
**Target reader:** Fleet owners and managers dealing with driver fraud or absconding incidents

---

It's one of the worst calls a fleet operator can receive. A driver who was working normally yesterday hasn't shown up today. His phone is off. The vehicle is gone. The guarantor's number rings out. You're left standing in your yard, wondering how it got to this point — and what you're supposed to do now.

This happens more than anyone in the industry wants to admit. The Nigeria Police Force's data on vehicle theft and commercial driver fraud shows this is one of the most common categories of fleet loss for small and mid-sized operators. And the majority of cases don't end in vehicle recovery.

This article won't undo the damage if you're already in this situation. But it will tell you what to do immediately, and — critically — how to close the gaps that made it possible in the first place.

## First 24 hours: what to do right now

**1. Call the next contact in the chain**

If the driver doesn't answer, go to the next number you have: the guarantor. If the guarantor doesn't answer, try any family contact you captured at onboarding. Do this before concluding anything — sometimes there's a medical emergency or breakdown and the driver has lost the ability to communicate. Rule out the benign explanations first.

**2. Check your last known location**

If you have any GPS or tracking on the vehicle, pull the last recorded position. If you've been dispatching digitally, when was the last assignment completed and where? Even a last-known address is somewhere to start.

**3. File a police report within 24 hours**

This is the step most operators delay, and it costs them. Go to the police station with:

- The vehicle's registration documents (make and keep copies)
- The driver's NIN, any ID documents you have on file
- The guarantor's details
- Any digital records of assignments, last communications, payments

The police report creates a formal record of the vehicle as stolen. This matters for insurance claims and for any subsequent recovery effort. The earlier you file, the better the paper trail.

**4. Alert your industry network**

Call other fleet operators you know. Share the driver's name, photo, and vehicle details. The informal network moves fast in this industry — a driver trying to sell a vehicle or find new work often surfaces within days of going missing.

## What to do in the first week

**5. Contact your insurance provider**

If the vehicle is insured, the claim process begins with the police report you filed. Follow up with your insurer and understand what documentation they need. If the vehicle isn't insured — which is a separate problem to address — the guarantor becomes your primary recovery channel.

**6. Pursue the guarantor formally**

If the guarantor is reachable, the conversation is straightforward: they signed a guarantee, the driver is gone, and they are now liable for the vehicle or its value. If you captured the guarantor properly — with a signed document, verified identity, and valid contact details — this is the moment that paperwork earns its value.

If you didn't capture the guarantor properly, this is much harder. You'll likely need a lawyer to have any formal standing.

**7. Engage a recovery service**

There are specialist vehicle recovery firms in Lagos and other major Nigerian cities that handle exactly this. They work through informant networks, police contacts, and mechanic/dealer channels where stolen vehicles often surface. The odds of recovery are higher if you act quickly and have any identifying information on the vehicle (engine number, chassis number, specific modifications or damage).

## The harder conversation: how did this happen?

Most driver absconding incidents follow a recognisable pattern in hindsight. Looking back, there were often signals:

- The driver had been submitting short remittances for two or three weeks without a clear dispute record
- The guarantor's details were never properly verified — the number worked at onboarding and hasn't been contacted since
- There was no face-match verification at onboarding — someone else's NIN may have been used
- The driver had a prior incident at another operator that you had no way to know about

None of this means you were negligent. It means you were operating without the infrastructure to catch these signals — which is where most of the industry has been.

## What a gap analysis looks like

Ask yourself these questions about your current setup:

**Identity:** Do you have a verified face match for every driver on your fleet — not just a photocopy of an ID, but a confirmed biometric match?

**Guarantor:** Is every guarantor's NIN on file and verified? Do you have a signed document making their liability explicit? Have you had any contact with them after onboarding to confirm they're still reachable?

**Remittance:** Can you pull a driver's full payment history — every confirmed payment, every shortfall, every dispute — in under two minutes? Or does that information live across three WhatsApp groups and someone's notebook?

**Cross-operator signals:** When you onboarded this driver, did any system tell you whether they had a prior absconding or fraud incident at another operator?

If the answer to most of these is no, you're carrying the same exposure that created the incident you're dealing with now.

## The economics of prevention

Replacing a vehicle lost to an absconder costs ₦3–15 million depending on type. Even partial loss of the vehicle's value, after recovery attempts, typically runs ₦1–5 million. The time spent on police follow-ups, guarantor negotiations, and insurance claims runs to dozens of hours over weeks.

The cost of building a proper system — verified onboarding, documented guarantors, remittance tracking, cross-operator checks — is a fraction of that. And unlike vehicle loss, it's fixed, not recurring.

One incident recovered because a system flagged a risk signal pays for years of operating that system.
