# The Guarantor Problem: Why It Fails (And How to Actually Fix It)

**Category:** Driver Management, Operations  
**Estimated read time:** 5 minutes  
**Target reader:** Fleet managers who use guarantors but have found the system unreliable

---

Almost every vehicle-for-hire operator in Nigeria uses guarantors. Almost every operator who has had a driver abscond has found that the guarantor was useless when it mattered.

This isn't a coincidence. The guarantor system, as most fleets run it today, is designed to feel like a safeguard while functioning like a formality. It protects against the honest driver who might feel socially accountable — but offers almost no protection against the driver who planned to disappear from the beginning.

Here's why, and what a guarantor system that actually works looks like.

## Why the standard approach fails

**The guarantor is chosen by the driver**

The driver picks his guarantor. He picks someone he knows, someone who will say yes, and — if he's planning ahead — someone who will be difficult to reach or who has nothing material to lose. The guarantor may not fully understand what they're agreeing to. They signed something in a rush at an onboarding meeting, or their name was added to a form they never saw.

This is a structural problem. A guarantor nominated and controlled by the person they're supposed to hold accountable isn't really a guarantor — it's a reference.

**The guarantor's identity is never verified**

When a driver brings a guarantor, how do you currently confirm the guarantor is who they say they are? In most operations: you don't. You take their name, a phone number, maybe a photocopy of an ID that you file away. You never independently check that the name on the ID matches a real person with real contact details.

This means a driver can nominate a fictional guarantor, or someone who has agreed to play the role and will become unreachable the moment the driver disappears.

**The document doesn't create real liability**

Most operators use some version of a guarantor form — a piece of paper the guarantor signs saying they're responsible if the driver runs. But a paper form with no clear financial amount, no witness, and no legal structure behind it isn't enforceable in any serious way. It creates a moral expectation, not a legal obligation.

When the guarantor disappears and you want to take action, your solicitor will tell you what you need to hear: the paperwork you have is difficult to use.

**There's no follow-up after onboarding**

A guarantor who is reachable on day one may not be reachable in month seven. Phone numbers change. People move. If the only time you've ever contacted the guarantor is when you need them to pay up, and you haven't verified they're still at the address or number on file, you're chasing a ghost.

## What a guarantor system that works actually looks like

**1. Verify the guarantor's identity independently**

The guarantor should go through the same basic identity check as the driver. At minimum: NIN verification — the NIMC database confirms the person's name and date of birth against their number, and produces the photo on file. Take a photo of the guarantor at onboarding. Compare it against the NIN record.

This eliminates fictional guarantors and borrowed identities. It also signals to the driver — before they start — that this process is serious.

**2. Capture BVN if the risk warrants it**

For higher-value vehicles or drivers without an established track record, capturing the guarantor's BVN creates a financial accountability link that is significantly harder to walk away from. BVN ties to a live bank account. When a guarantor knows you have their BVN on file, the calculus of becoming unreachable changes.

**3. Make the liability explicit and documented**

The guarantor agreement should state, clearly and specifically:

- What they are guaranteeing (the vehicle make, model, and registration)
- The amount they are liable for (either the vehicle's value or a fixed amount)
- The conditions under which that liability is triggered
- That they understand and agree to be contacted if the driver defaults

This document should be witnessed, dated, and stored digitally — not in a physical folder that can be lost.

**4. Build in a mid-tenure check-in**

Six months into a driver's tenure, send the guarantor a message. Not to alarm them — just to confirm contact details are still valid and they're aware the driver is still active. This confirms reachability before you need it, and it reminds the guarantor that the agreement is live, not expired.

**5. The guarantor process should be part of driver onboarding, not a box to tick at the end**

When a driver completes onboarding and sees that the platform has verified their guarantor's identity, captured their financial details, and created a documented liability agreement, they understand something important: this is not the same as the old system. That understanding changes their behaviour.

The purpose of a good guarantor process isn't just recovery — it's deterrence. A driver who knows you have a real paper trail is significantly less likely to test it.

## What to do if you're already carrying weak guarantors

If your current fleet has drivers onboarded under the old approach — paper forms, unverified identities, numbers that may or may not still work — you have a few options.

The most practical one: make it a fleet-wide update. Send a communication to all drivers saying you're moving to a new onboarding and compliance system, and that all drivers will need to complete an updated guarantor registration as part of that process. Most drivers will comply. The ones who resist most strongly are often the ones worth looking at more carefully.

Use this moment to capture what you should have had from the start: verified guarantor identities, proper documentation, and a system that can tell you, at any time, who each guarantor is and whether they're still reachable.
