# Is Your Fleet Actually Profitable? How to Calculate Per-Vehicle Economics

**Category:** Finance, Operations  
**Estimated read time:** 6 minutes  
**Target reader:** Fleet owners who want to understand which vehicles (and drivers) are actually making money

---

Most fleet operators know whether the business overall is making money. What most don't know is *which vehicles* are making money — and which are quietly running at a loss while the profitable units carry them.

This distinction matters enormously. When you average out fleet performance, the strong units hide the weak ones. A fleet of 50 vehicles where 35 are profitable and 15 are losing money can still show a positive bottom line — right up until a market shift or a few bad months push you into trouble.

The operators who grow consistently are the ones who measure per-vehicle economics and manage by unit, not just by total.

Here's how to do the calculation — and what to do with the results.

## The per-vehicle P&L

For each vehicle in your fleet, you need three numbers:

**Revenue:** What the vehicle is supposed to generate in remittance per month. This is your target remittance rate × operating days. Example: ₦10,000/day × 26 days = ₦260,000/month target.

**Collected:** What the vehicle actually generated — confirmed remittances, minus waived or disputed amounts that were written off. This is the number most operators don't have clearly, because remittance tracking is informal.

**Cost:** The direct costs attributable to that vehicle each month. This typically includes:
- Financing or depreciation (if you borrowed to buy it)
- Insurance
- Routine maintenance and tyres (amortised monthly)
- Any direct staff costs tied to that vehicle (e.g. a dedicated dispatcher)

The difference between Collected and Cost is your per-vehicle contribution margin — the amount each vehicle is actually putting in your pocket.

## A realistic worked example

Let's take Vehicle A: a 2019 Hiace bus, purchased for ₦8 million, currently operating in Lagos.

**Monthly target remittance:** ₦260,000 (₦10,000 × 26 days)

**Average monthly collected** (based on last 3 months): ₦218,000 — a 16% shortfall, which tracks with typical leakage in an untracked fleet.

**Monthly costs:**
- Loan repayment: ₦72,000
- Insurance: ₦12,000
- Maintenance reserve (₦1,500/day average): ₦39,000
- **Total costs: ₦123,000**

**Contribution margin:** ₦218,000 − ₦123,000 = **₦95,000/month**

That looks reasonable. But now run the same number assuming you closed the collection gap:

If your fleet was collecting at 98% instead of 84%, monthly collected goes to ₦254,800.

**Contribution margin at 98%:** ₦254,800 − ₦123,000 = **₦131,800/month**

That's ₦36,800 more per vehicle, per month. On 40 vehicles, that's ₦1.47 million per month in margin that you're currently leaving in the gap between target and actual.

## The vehicles that look fine but aren't

The exercise above becomes more revealing when you apply it across the whole fleet and sort by actual contribution margin, not assumed.

Typically, when operators do this analysis for the first time, they find:

**High-target, low-collection vehicles** — assigned to experienced drivers who know how to manage the argument. They remit enough to avoid confrontation but consistently short by 10–15%. Because the shortfall per day seems small, nobody focuses on it. Over a year, it's significant.

**High-maintenance vehicles** — older units where maintenance reserve keeps getting exceeded. The remittance is fine but the cost side is eating the margin. These vehicles may need to be retired or refinanced.

**Under-assigned vehicles** — vehicles that should be generating 26 operating days per month but are actually operating 18–20 days, because assignment tracking is informal and gaps go unnoticed. The vehicle isn't making money; you just don't know exactly why.

**Consistently disputed vehicles** — a specific vehicle has a disproportionate number of remittance disputes attached to it. That might be a bad driver, a bad route, or a problem with the vehicle itself. Without the dispute data, you can't tell.

## What you need to do this analysis

This calculation requires data that, for most operators, exists in fragments or not at all:

- **Actual collected remittance per vehicle, per month** (not estimated — confirmed)
- **Dispute and waiver records** (what was written off and why)
- **Operating days per vehicle** (was the vehicle actually on the road?)
- **Maintenance logs** (what did you spend on each vehicle?)

If your remittance tracking is in a notebook or WhatsApp, you won't have reliable numbers for the first three items. You'll be estimating — and estimating tends to be optimistic in ways that look fine on a spreadsheet but feel wrong at the end of the month.

## The management habit that changes everything

The operators who run tightest on margin don't necessarily have different vehicles or different routes. They have a weekly habit: they look at last week's remittance against target for every vehicle, flag any vehicle below 90% of expected, and investigate within 48 hours.

This sounds simple because it is. The hard part isn't the logic — it's having the data to do it consistently without it taking three hours every Friday.

When remittance is tracked at the point of collection — confirmed or disputed in real time, by the driver, with a timestamp — the weekly review takes ten minutes instead of three hours. You open the dashboard. You see the numbers. You make the calls.

That's the operational difference between a fleet that grows and one that plateaus.

## A starting point if you don't have the data yet

If you can't currently pull per-vehicle collection data for last month, start with a simpler question: take your total expected remittance for the fleet last month and compare it to total confirmed collected. The gap is your minimum leakage number.

Then identify your five highest-risk vehicles by gut feel — the ones where you've had the most disputes, the drivers you trust least, the oldest units costing the most in maintenance. Run the per-vehicle calculation for those five. That will tell you whether the problem is concentrated or distributed across the fleet.

Either way, you'll know something you didn't know before you started.
