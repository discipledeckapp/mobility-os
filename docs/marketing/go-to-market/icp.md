# Ideal Customer Profile

---

## Primary ICP — Mid-Market Vehicle-for-Hire Operator

### Firmographics

| Attribute | Range / Description |
|---|---|
| Fleet size | 20–200 vehicles |
| Driver count | 25–250 |
| Business type | Bus/minibus operator, ride-hail fleet partner, corporate shuttle, motorcycle/tricycle fleet at scale |
| Revenue model | Daily driver remittance (driver pays owner per day for use of the vehicle) |
| Geography | Lagos, Abuja, Port Harcourt (Nigeria primary); Accra, Kumasi (Ghana); Nairobi, Mombasa (Kenya); Johannesburg, Cape Town (South Africa) |
| Stage | Established (2+ years operating), not pre-revenue |
| Management | Has at least one dedicated fleet/operations manager; owner is not the sole operator |

### The Vehicle-for-Hire Economics

This is what makes the ICP distinct from a corporate fleet or logistics operator:

- **Daily remittance model**: The driver pays the owner ₦7,500–₦25,000/day (Nigeria), KES 1,500–4,000/day (Kenya), GHS 80–200/day (Ghana) for the right to operate the vehicle. The driver keeps revenue above that threshold.
- **Cash-first**: Remittance is almost always cash, collected at a depot or meeting point each morning.
- **Driver as independent contractor**: The driver bears fuel cost and minor maintenance. The owner provides the vehicle and registration.
- **Guarantor culture**: Most operators require a guarantor for a driver — someone who is financially liable if the driver absconds with the vehicle. This is informal, undocumented, and unverifiable in the current market.

### Triggers (Why They Buy Now)

These are the events that create urgent buying intent:

| Trigger | Description |
|---|---|
| **Fraud incident** | A driver absconded with the vehicle, or remittance was systematically under-reported. After this, operators want a paper trail. |
| **Scale breakpoint** | Growing past 20–30 vehicles where WhatsApp groups and notebooks stop working. |
| **Investor / bank demand** | A lender (for fleet expansion) or investor wants to see operational records, compliance documentation, or a management system. |
| **Regulatory pressure** | LASG digital vehicle registration, NTSA TIMS requirements, or an NDPA compliance question from a driver. |
| **New operations manager** | A new hire who comes from a more formal background insists on a proper system. |
| **Guarantor dispute** | A driver abandons a vehicle and the guarantor cannot be found — because guarantor records were not systematically captured. |

### Buyer Persona

**Primary buyer title:** Fleet Manager, Head of Operations, General Manager

**Also involved:** MD/Owner (final sign-off), Finance Manager (billing approval for larger deals)

**Day-to-day pain:**
- Chasing 50 drivers every morning to confirm remittance. No record of who paid, disputed, or is outstanding.
- Being told by a driver "I paid yesterday" with no way to verify.
- Not knowing a driver's licence expired until they're stopped at an FRSC checkpoint.
- Onboarding a new driver: collecting photocopies, calling references, hoping the guarantor is real.
- No way to know which of your vehicles is currently active, idle, or in an assignment.

**What they want:**
- One place where they can see every driver, every vehicle, and today's assignment status.
- Remittance disputes resolved with a record, not an argument.
- Driver onboarding that the driver mostly completes themselves — no paper-chasing.
- A compliance report they can hand to a bank, a regulator, or a new investor.

### Where to Find Them

- **National Union of Road Transport Workers (NURTW)** and **Road Transport Employers Association of Nigeria (RTEAN)** networks — Nigeria
- **GPRTU (Ghana Private Road Transport Union)** and **GHARTEP** — Ghana
- **Matatu Owners Association of Kenya (MOAK)** and **Matatu SACCOs** — Kenya
- **South African National Taxi Council (SANTACO)** — South Africa
- LinkedIn search: "Fleet Manager Nigeria", "Transport Manager Nairobi"
- Uber/Bolt fleet partner communities and groups
- Commercial vehicle dealer networks (dealers know who the fleet buyers are)

## Secondary ICP — Ride-Hail Fleet Partner

### Profile

- Owns 10–100 vehicles, all operating on Uber, Bolt, or InDrive
- Revenue model: takes a cut of driver weekly earnings (not daily remittance)
- Has compliance requirements from the platform (Uber/Bolt require driver documentation)
- Often managed with a small team of 2–5 people

### Why Mobiris Fits

- Driver verification requirements from Uber/Bolt align with Mobiris's onboarding workflow
- Assignment tracking and driver status management are immediately valuable
- Moove-financed fleets (Moove is the largest vehicle financing player in this space, operating in Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Egypt) are natural Mobiris prospects — Moove provides the vehicle, operators need the management layer

### Differentiation in pitch

For this segment, emphasise the **driver compliance module** and **mobile field app** over remittance (their remittance flows through the platform, but driver records are still a pain point).

## Tertiary ICP — Enterprise Transport Group

### Profile

- Holding company or franchisor with multiple subsidiary operators
- 200+ vehicles, multi-city or multi-country presence
- Needs consolidated reporting across subsidiaries
- Deal motion: top-down, ARR contract, procurement process

### Examples of this profile

- A family group that owns bus routes in Lagos, Abuja, and Port Harcourt under different operating names
- A corporate shuttle company managing commutes for 10+ enterprise clients (Treepz/Plentywaka model)
- A logistics company with a driver fleet alongside cargo vehicles

### What they need beyond the primary ICP

- Multi-entity hierarchy (holding company → subsidiary → fleet)
- Cross-entity reporting and compliance view
- Custom billing terms and invoicing
- SLA guarantee and dedicated support contact
- Data Processing Agreement

## Anti-ICP (Do Not Pursue)

| Profile | Why |
|---|---|
| **Solo/micro-operator (1–5 vehicles)** | ACV too low (<$30/month). High churn. Likely WhatsApp-forever. |
| **Pure long-haul trucking / freight** | No driver remittance model. Kobo360 / Lori Systems serve this segment. |
| **Consumer ride-hailing startup** | Building tech themselves or using Uber/Bolt platform. Wrong product. |
| **Government / parastatal fleet** | 12–24 month procurement cycles. Budget unpredictability. Different compliance regime. |
| **Motorcycle taxi (okada) at micro scale** | Informal, low-margin, regulatory hostility in key markets (Lagos ban). |

## Qualification Framework

Use these questions to qualify an inbound or outreach prospect in under 10 minutes:

1. How many vehicles are you currently running, and what type? (Minibus, sedan, truck?)
2. How do drivers pay you — daily remittance, weekly, or revenue share?
3. How do you track that today — spreadsheet, notebook, WhatsApp?
4. When did you last onboard a new driver? How long did it take?
5. Have you had a remittance dispute or a driver fraud incident in the past 12 months?
6. Do you have a dedicated fleet/operations manager, or do you manage this yourself?

**Red flags:** Solo operator. Pure freight. Government contract work. No remittance model.

**Green flags:** 20+ vehicles. Daily remittance collection. Recent fraud incident. Growing fleet. Investor or compliance pressure.
